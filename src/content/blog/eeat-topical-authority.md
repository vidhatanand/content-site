EEAT and topical authority are the two most consequential quality signals in Google's content evaluation framework right now. They're related — but they're not the same thing. Confusing them leads to content strategies that get one right and fail the other.

This article explains what each signal actually measures, where they intersect, and what Google is looking for in content that scores high on both. We'll go beyond what's in Google's public documentation and into what the top-ranking content in competitive informational categories actually demonstrates.

---

## What EEAT Actually Measures

EEAT stands for Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. It's Google's quality rater framework — the criteria human quality raters use when evaluating whether search results are serving users well. While rater evaluations don't directly influence ranking algorithms, they inform and validate the machine learning systems that do.

Here's what each dimension actually means in practice:

### Experience

Experience is the newest addition (the first E was added in December 2022) and the most misunderstood. It asks: does this content demonstrate that the author has personally done, used, or encountered what they're writing about?

For a product review: did the reviewer actually use the product, or is this synthesized from other reviews?
For a how-to guide: is the advice based on the author's direct implementation, or is it general advice that could have come from anywhere?
For a case study: are the results real, specific, and verifiable — or are they hypothetical?

Experience is about first-person evidence. It's not satisfied by credentials or third-party citations alone — you need to show the work.

### Expertise

Expertise asks: does the content demonstrate deep knowledge of the subject, beyond what a general reader could assemble from the top 10 search results?

Expertise is visible in:
- Correct use of domain-specific terminology and nuance
- Recognition of edge cases and exceptions that generalist coverage ignores
- Specific, concrete recommendations rather than vague principle-level advice
- Accurate citation of primary sources (research, official documentation, direct data)

The bar for expertise varies dramatically by topic. YMYL topics (health, finance, legal) require credentialed expertise. For most informational content, demonstrated domain knowledge is sufficient.

### Authoritativeness

Authoritativeness is primarily an off-page signal: how does the broader web — other sites, publications, industry voices — evaluate the credibility of your content and your site?

In practice: do other sites in your topic area link to you as a source? Do authoritative publications in your industry reference your work? Are you cited by sources that themselves have established authority?

This is where topical authority and EEAT directly intersect. A site with deep topical authority — comprehensive cluster coverage on a subject — naturally becomes a reference resource that other sites link to. Those links are the external signal that validates authoritativeness.

### Trustworthiness

Trustworthiness is the foundation. Without it, the other three signals don't matter. It asks: can users trust that this site and this content are what they appear to be?

Trust signals include: accurate author attribution, transparent editorial processes, factual accuracy, no deceptive patterns, clear separation of editorial and commercial content, functioning contact information, and site security (HTTPS).

Negative trust signals: anonymous authorship on YMYL topics, no citations for factual claims, ads that look like content, and patterns of inaccuracy.

## What Topical Authority Measures

Where EEAT evaluates the *quality* of individual content pieces and their authors, topical authority evaluates the *coverage completeness* of a site on a subject.

Topical authority asks: has this site systematically covered the major subtopics, questions, and dimensions of a subject area? Or does it have one good article in a sea of unrelated content?

The mechanism: Google evaluates topical authority through the structure of your content — how many pieces cover the topic, how thoroughly each covers its subtopic, and how those pieces link to each other. A site with 15 interlinked articles on technical SEO sends a stronger topical signal than a site with one excellent article on the same topic.

## Where EEAT and Topical Authority Intersect

The intersection is where ranking performance actually happens. Neither signal alone is sufficient for durable first-page rankings in competitive informational categories.

**EEAT without topical authority:** You have high-quality content on some topics, but thin or absent coverage on adjacent subtopics. Google recognizes the quality of individual pages but can't classify your site as a comprehensive authority on the subject. Result: ranking for the specific keywords you target, but no compounding cluster benefit and vulnerability to topically comprehensive competitors.

**Topical authority without EEAT:** You've published 15 articles in a cluster, but they're thin, generic, or AI-generated without expert review. Google sees the coverage breadth but evaluates the individual pieces as low-quality. Result: initial indexing, possibly brief ranking, then degradation as Google's quality systems catch up.

**Both together:** Comprehensive coverage of a topical domain, where each piece demonstrates first-person experience, domain expertise, external citations, and transparent authorship. This is what produces durable, compounding organic rankings.

## What Google Actually Rewards in 2026

Here's the practical synthesis — what the content that ranks in the top 3 positions on competitive informational keywords actually demonstrates, based on observing hundreds of competitive SERPs:

**1. Specific, non-generic advice.** Not "create high-quality content." Instead: "For a cluster of 15 articles, the minimum internal link density that produces measurable topical signal improvement in GSC is 45–55 total internal links within the cluster." Specificity is a credibility signal. Vague advice is easy to generate; specific operational guidance demonstrates that someone has actually done this.

**2. Counterintuitive or contrarian claims, backed by evidence.** Top-ranking content often includes claims that contradict conventional wisdom — and cites data or personal experience to back them. This signals genuine expertise (you have opinions based on direct experience) and generates engagement signals (readers spend time, share, link).

**3. Acknowledgment of limitations and tradeoffs.** Content that presents one-sided "here's how to do X" without acknowledging when X doesn't work, when the tradeoffs aren't worth it, or when the advice applies to some contexts and not others — this signals inexperience. Expert content acknowledges complexity.

**4. Updated recency signals.** Google explicitly rewards freshness on time-sensitive topics. Articles updated with current data, statistics, and examples — and that show a "last updated" date — outperform unchanged articles from prior years on the same keywords.

**5. Named authors with verifiable credentials.** Especially on topics where expertise matters (health, finance, legal, B2B technical), named authors with linked author pages, professional profiles, and external mentions outperform anonymous "team" authorship.

**6. Comprehensive subtopic coverage.** For informational content: does this article answer every major question a reader entering with this search query might have? Articles that leave obvious follow-up questions unanswered lose to articles that anticipate and address them.

## The EEAT Checklist for Cluster Content

When building a topical authority cluster, apply this checklist to each article before publication:

**Experience:**
- [ ] Does this article reference our direct experience with this topic?
- [ ] Are there specific examples, data points, or case studies from our own work?
- [ ] Is there anything in this article that could only have been written by someone who has actually done this?

**Expertise:**
- [ ] Does this article demonstrate knowledge beyond what's in the current top 10 results?
- [ ] Are claims backed by citations to primary sources?
- [ ] Are there specific nuances, edge cases, or operational details that a generalist wouldn't know?

**Authoritativeness:**
- [ ] Is there a named author with a linked author page?
- [ ] Does the author page reference credentials, publications, or other work?
- [ ] Are there inbound links from topically relevant sources pointing to this article or site?

**Trustworthiness:**
- [ ] Are factual claims cited?
- [ ] Is the content accurate and free of misleading claims?
- [ ] Is the site's authorship and editorial process transparent?

## The Most Common EEAT Failures in Content Clusters

**The "summarize the top 10" pattern.** AI-generated or low-effort content that synthesizes existing articles without adding original perspective, data, or experience. This content may initially rank (if topical authority exists) but degrades as Google's quality systems evaluate engagement signals.

**Generic author attribution.** "Written by the Editorial Team" satisfies no EEAT signal. It's not wrong, but it's a missed opportunity. Named authors with profiles, relevant credentials, and consistent publication histories outperform anonymous content in competitive informational categories.

**No first-person evidence.** An article about "how to build topical authority" that doesn't reference actual clusters built, actual ranking outcomes, or actual internal link architectures is advice, not experience. Advice is easy to produce. Experience is what EEAT is looking for.

**Outdated statistics cited without update.** Citing a 2021 study in a 2026 article on Google's ranking behavior without acknowledging that things have changed is a trust signal failure. If you reference data, either use current sources or explicitly note the limitations of older data.

## How to Apply EEAT to Your Cluster Strategy

The practical implication is this: EEAT requirements need to be baked into your content briefs, not applied as a post-publication checklist.

Before writing each cluster article, answer:
1. What specific first-person experience do we have with this subtopic?
2. What data, case studies, or outcomes from our own work can we reference?
3. What does an expert know about this topic that a generalist doesn't?
4. What external sources (primary research, industry publications, official documentation) should this article cite?

These answers go into the brief. The writer — human or AI-assisted — then has the material to produce content that meets the EEAT bar, not just content that fills the word count.
