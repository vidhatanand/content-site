Most marketers using AI get mediocre output — not because the AI is bad, but because their prompts are. The difference between a generic AI response and copy that actually converts comes down almost entirely to how you prompt.

This guide gives you 20+ battle-tested prompt templates for the most common marketing copy formats, plus the underlying principles that make them work. By the end, you'll have a reusable prompt library that cuts your copy production time in half while improving quality.

## The Anatomy of a High-Converting Marketing Prompt

Before the templates, you need the principles. Every high-quality marketing prompt shares five elements:

1. **Role assignment** — Tell the AI who it's being. "You are an expert B2B SaaS copywriter with 10 years of experience writing for technical buyers" produces better output than "write me a subject line."

2. **Context and constraints** — Product, audience, tone, word count, platform. The more specific, the better.

3. **Goal and intent** — What should the copy *do*? Drive a click? Overcome an objection? Create urgency?

4. **Format specification** — Output structure, length, what to include or avoid.

5. **Examples or inspiration** — One or two examples of the style you're aiming for dramatically improve output quality.

## Email Subject Line Templates

### Template 1: Curiosity-Gap Subject Line

```
You are an email marketing copywriter specializing in B2B SaaS.

Write 10 subject lines for an email about [TOPIC] to [AUDIENCE].

Requirements:
- Use curiosity gaps (imply there's something they don't know yet)
- 6–9 words each
- No clickbait — the email must deliver on the subject line's promise
- Avoid spam trigger words (free, guaranteed, urgent)
- Mix statement and question formats

Product context: [PRODUCT NAME] helps [ICP] achieve [OUTCOME].
```

### Template 2: Pain-Point Subject Line

```
Act as a direct-response email copywriter.

Write 8 subject lines that speak directly to this pain point: [PAIN POINT]

Target persona: [DESCRIBE BUYER PERSONA]
Stage of funnel: [AWARENESS / CONSIDERATION / DECISION]
Tone: [CONVERSATIONAL / PROFESSIONAL / PUNCHY]

Each subject line should:
- Name the problem without solving it (save the solution for the body)
- Be under 50 characters for mobile preview
- Feel like it's from a human, not a brand
```

### Template 3: Social Proof Subject Line

```
Write 6 subject lines that use social proof to build credibility.

Product: [PRODUCT]
Recent win or stat: [E.g., "customers reduced churn by 34%"]
Audience: [AUDIENCE]

Format: Lead with the result, hint at the method. Example structure: "[Result] — here's how [Company/Customer] did it"
```

## Landing Page Copy Templates

### Template 4: Hero Section Copy

```
You are a conversion copywriter specializing in SaaS landing pages.

Write a hero section for a [PRODUCT TYPE] landing page.

Include:
- H1 headline (outcome-focused, not feature-focused, max 10 words)
- H2 subheadline (expands on H1, addresses the "how" or "who it's for", 1-2 sentences)
- 3-bullet benefit list (each bullet: [pain point avoided] + [outcome gained])
- CTA button text (action-oriented, value-focused, 3-5 words)

Context:
- Product: [PRODUCT NAME] — [ONE-LINE DESCRIPTION]
- Target buyer: [ICP]
- Primary outcome: [WHAT THEY ACHIEVE]
- Key differentiator: [WHAT MAKES IT DIFFERENT]
- Tone: [CONFIDENT / FRIENDLY / AUTHORITATIVE]
```

### Template 5: Feature-to-Benefit Translation

```
Take this list of product features and rewrite each as a customer-centric benefit.

Features:
1. [FEATURE 1]
2. [FEATURE 2]
3. [FEATURE 3]

For each, write:
- The feature (what it is)
- The benefit (what it does for the customer)
- The emotional outcome (how it makes them feel / what it enables)

Format as a three-column table. Audience: [AUDIENCE]. Tone: [TONE].
```

### Template 6: Objection-Handling Section

```
Write an FAQ/objection-handling section for a landing page.

Product: [PRODUCT]
Top 5 objections from sales calls:
1. [OBJECTION 1]
2. [OBJECTION 2]
3. [OBJECTION 3]
4. [OBJECTION 4]
5. [OBJECTION 5]

For each objection:
- Write the question as a skeptical buyer would ask it
- Write a 2-3 sentence response that acknowledges the concern, provides evidence, and redirects to the benefit

Tone: Direct and confident, never defensive.
```

## Ad Copy Templates

### Template 7: Google Search Ad

```
Write 3 Google Search Ad variations for [KEYWORD].

For each variation, provide:
- Headline 1 (30 chars max): [Lead with keyword]
- Headline 2 (30 chars max): [Key benefit or differentiator]
- Headline 3 (30 chars max): [CTA or social proof]
- Description 1 (90 chars max): [Problem + solution]
- Description 2 (90 chars max): [Benefit + CTA]

Target keyword: [KEYWORD]
Landing page goal: [GOAL]
Unique value proposition: [UVP]
```

### Template 8: LinkedIn Ad Copy

```
You are a B2B paid social copywriter.

Write a LinkedIn single-image ad for [PRODUCT/OFFER].

Format:
- Hook line (first 2 lines visible before "see more"): Create immediate relevance for [JOB TITLE] at [COMPANY TYPE]
- Body (3-4 short paragraphs): Problem → Agitate → Solution → Proof
- CTA: [BUTTON TEXT] — make it specific to the offer, not generic

Constraints:
- 150 words max
- No jargon. Write at a 9th-grade reading level.
- One idea per paragraph
- Use line breaks aggressively for scannability

Audience: [AUDIENCE]. Offer: [OFFER]. Tone: [TONE].
```

### Template 9: Facebook/Meta Retargeting Ad

```
Write a retargeting ad for people who visited [PAGE URL TYPE] but didn't convert.

Assume they:
- Know what the product does
- Have a specific objection or hesitation (address this)
- Respond to: urgency, social proof, or risk reversal

Product: [PRODUCT]
Most common drop-off reason: [REASON]
Offer or incentive: [INCENTIVE, or "none"]

Write 3 variations:
1. Urgency-focused
2. Testimonial/social proof-focused
3. Risk-reversal/guarantee-focused

Each: 50–75 words body copy + CTA.
```

## Email Body Copy Templates

### Template 10: Cold Outreach Email

```
Write a cold outreach email for [PRODUCT/OFFER].

Hard rules:
- 3 paragraphs max, 150 words total
- Paragraph 1: Relevance hook (why THIS person, not generic)
- Paragraph 2: One specific problem they likely have + one-line solution
- Paragraph 3: Single, low-friction CTA (not "let's hop on a call" — something easier)
- No "I hope this finds you well"
- No feature lists
- Write as if from a human, not a company

Personalization token: [WHAT YOU KNOW ABOUT THEM / THEIR COMPANY]
Product: [PRODUCT]
ICP: [IDEAL CUSTOMER PROFILE]
```

### Template 11: Nurture Sequence Email

```
Write Email [NUMBER] in a [X]-part nurture sequence.

Sequence goal: Move [AUDIENCE] from [CURRENT STAGE] to [DESIRED ACTION]

This email's job: [SPECIFIC GOAL FOR THIS EMAIL — e.g., "overcome the 'it's too expensive' objection"]

Include:
- Subject line (3 options)
- Preview text (1 option, 40–50 characters)
- Body (200–300 words)
  - Opening that connects to previous email or reader's situation
  - Main content block
  - Soft CTA

Tone: [TONE]. Brand voice notes: [ANY SPECIFIC VOICE GUIDELINES].
```

## Social Media Copy Templates

### Template 12: LinkedIn Thought Leadership Post

```
Write a LinkedIn post for [TOPIC] from the perspective of [PERSONA/ROLE].

Structure:
- Hook (first line — must stop the scroll, no clickbait, no "I have news")
- 3-5 short paragraphs making one clear argument or sharing one insight
- Takeaway or question to drive comments

Constraints:
- Use line breaks every 1-2 sentences
- No buzzwords: "exciting," "thrilled," "game-changer," "leverage"
- Write in first person
- Under 250 words

Topic: [TOPIC]
POV: [CONTRARIAN / CONFIRMATORY / HOW-TO / STORY]
```

### Template 13: Twitter/X Thread

```
Write a Twitter thread about [TOPIC].

Format:
- Tweet 1: Hook — make a bold, specific claim that promises value
- Tweets 2-8: Each tweet delivers one actionable insight or example
- Tweet 9: Summary + CTA (follow, share, or link)

Rules:
- Each tweet max 240 characters
- No "a thread 🧵" openers
- Lead every tweet with the most important information
- Use numbers for lists

Topic: [TOPIC]. Audience: [AUDIENCE]. Goal: [GOAL].
```

## Advanced Prompting Techniques

### Chain-of-Thought Prompting for Complex Copy

For complex copy that requires nuance — like positioning pages or high-stakes sales emails — add a reasoning step before generation:

```
Before writing, answer these questions in 1-2 sentences each:
1. What is the #1 thing this reader cares about?
2. What is their biggest objection to buying?
3. What would make this reader trust this claim?
4. What is the single most important thing they should believe after reading this?

Now, write [COPY TYPE] using your answers to inform every word.
```

### Persona-Driven Variation

When you need multiple versions for different segments:

```
Write 3 versions of [COPY TYPE] for the same product, one for each of these personas:

Persona A: [DESCRIPTION]
Persona B: [DESCRIPTION]
Persona C: [DESCRIPTION]

For each persona, emphasize the pain point and outcome most relevant to them. Keep the core offer identical — change only the framing.
```

### Iteration Prompt: Punching Up Weak Copy

When you have a draft that's technically correct but lacks punch:

```
Here is a piece of marketing copy: [PASTE COPY]

Critique it against these criteria:
1. Does the headline make a specific, believable promise?
2. Does every sentence earn its place?
3. Is there a clear, singular CTA?
4. Does it sound like a human or a press release?

Rewrite it to fix the weakest 3 elements. Don't change what's working.
```

## Building Your Prompt Library

The real leverage in prompt engineering for marketing copy comes from systematizing. Don't use these templates once — build them into your workflow:

1. **Create a shared doc** with your team's most-used templates, customized for your product and voice.
2. **Add brand voice notes** to every template (see our guide on [training AI on your brand voice](/blog/train-ai-brand-voice)).
3. **Version-control your prompts** — when output quality drops (often after model updates), you need a baseline to compare against.
4. **Track winning prompts** — note which templates produce copy that gets approved without major edits.

The teams that win with AI-assisted copywriting aren't using fancier AI. They're using better, more systematized prompts.
