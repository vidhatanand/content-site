Most topical authority strategies that aren't producing results have a diagnosable failure point. They're not failing because the strategy is wrong — they're failing because of a specific gap in execution: a missing pillar page, an insufficient internal link map, a keyword distribution skewed toward high-difficulty terms, or a coverage gap that leaves a subtopic unaddressed.

This 40-question checklist is designed to find that failure point. Work through each section systematically. Every "no" answer is an action item. When you reach the end, you'll have a prioritized list of the interventions most likely to improve your results.

---

## Section 1: Cluster Architecture (Questions 1–8)

**1. Does your cluster have a dedicated pillar page?**

The pillar page is the hub that defines the cluster's topical scope and links to all supporting articles. Without it, the cluster lacks structural coherence.

- ✅ Yes: The pillar is published and links to all supporting articles
- ⚠️ Partial: The pillar exists but doesn't link to all supporting articles
- ❌ No: No pillar page exists — this is your first action item

**2. Does your pillar page cover all major subtopics at an introductory level?**

A pillar page that only covers some subtopics signals incomplete topical authority. Every major subtopic should have at least a paragraph of coverage in the pillar, with a link to the dedicated supporting article.

**3. Is the pillar page comprehensive (3,000+ words)?**

Short pillar pages lack the depth needed to serve as an authoritative hub. Target 3,500–5,000 words for competitive subjects.

**4. How many supporting articles does your cluster have?**

- ✅ 12+ articles: Above the typical authority threshold for moderate-competition subjects
- ⚠️ 8–11 articles: Partial signal — may not reach full topical authority in competitive subjects
- ❌ < 8 articles: Below the coverage threshold for most subjects — add articles before expecting cluster authority effects

**5. Do your supporting articles cover all major subtopics in the subject area?**

Audit this by comparing your published article list against a comprehensive list of subtopics (use Semrush's Keyword Magic Tool or Google's "People Also Ask" for the subject). Every major subtopic should have a dedicated article.

**6. Does your cluster address all three content types: foundational, practical, and commercial?**

- ✅ All three: Complete cluster content mix
- ⚠️ Two of three: Partial mix — identify which is missing and add it
- ❌ One or none: Major coverage gap

**7. Is the pillar page the most authoritative, comprehensive piece in the cluster?**

The pillar should not be the shortest or thinnest article in the cluster. If a supporting article is more comprehensive than the pillar, restructure — the supporting article may actually be your true pillar.

**8. Do you have internal links from the pillar to every supporting article?**

Every published supporting article should have a corresponding link in the pillar page. Unpublished articles can have placeholder references. Audit this before each new content publication.

## Section 2: Keyword Strategy (Questions 9–16)

**9. Does your cluster have keywords across all three KD tiers?**

- ✅ Tier 1 (KD 15–30), Tier 2 (KD 31–42), Tier 3 (KD 43+) all represented
- ⚠️ Only one or two tiers
- ❌ Cluster is KD 40+ throughout — need foundational low-KD content

**10. Did you publish low-KD articles first (weeks 1–2)?**

Publishing high-KD articles before establishing topical signal with low-KD content significantly delays ranking movement. Evaluate your publication order — if high-KD articles published first, the cluster may need time to recover.

**11. Does your pillar page target a head term that matches the cluster's primary subject?**

The pillar page's primary keyword should be the broadest, most definitional term for the cluster. Not a long-tail variant, not a specific subtopic keyword.

**12. Are there significant search volume gaps in your keyword coverage?**

If the highest-volume subtopics in your subject area don't have dedicated articles, you have a major coverage gap. Identify the top 5 highest-volume subtopics and verify each has an article.

**13. Do any articles in your cluster cannibalize each other's keywords?**

Multiple articles targeting the same keyword or highly similar intent signals compete against each other and dilute your cluster's authority. Audit for overlapping intent across articles.

**14. Does each article target a specific keyword (not just a topic)?**

Generic topic coverage ("an article about internal linking") is weaker than a specific keyword-targeted article ("Internal Linking Strategy for Topical Authority: The Complete Playbook"). Every article should have a primary keyword with measurable search volume.

**15. Do your article titles include the target keyword?**

The simplest on-page signal. Titles that don't include the primary keyword reduce relevance signals without any compensating benefit.

**16. Are your meta descriptions written for click-through, not just keywords?**

Meta descriptions don't directly affect ranking, but they affect click-through rate, which does. Audit meta descriptions across the cluster — are they compelling enough to earn the click when your article appears in position 8 vs. a competitor in position 6?

## Section 3: Internal Linking (Questions 17–22)

**17. Does every cluster article link back to the pillar page?**

Non-negotiable. Every article in the cluster must link to the pillar. This is the most important internal link requirement for topical authority.

**18. Does every article link to 3–5 directly related cluster articles?**

Beyond the pillar link, each article should link to the most topically adjacent cluster articles. A total of 4–6 internal links per article (including the pillar) is the standard.

**19. Does the pillar page link back to all published supporting articles?**

Verify this with a manual audit — pillar pages that haven't been updated as new articles publish are a common coverage gap.

**20. Are internal links contextually embedded in the content?**

Links placed in isolation ("See also:") have less SEO value than links embedded naturally in a sentence where the link text describes the linked content ("For the full technical approach to building internal link architecture, see..."). Audit link placement quality.

**21. Do higher-authority existing articles link to new cluster articles?**

When you publish a new cluster article, add internal links from already-indexed, higher-performing cluster articles to the new article. This accelerates discovery and passes link equity.

**22. Is the total internal link count within the cluster above the threshold?**

For a 12–18 article cluster, target 50–110 total internal links within the cluster. Below 30 links indicates insufficient internal link density.

## Section 4: Content Quality (Questions 23–30)

**23. Does each article fully satisfy the search intent for its target keyword?**

Audit your highest-impression articles in Google Search Console. If articles have high impressions but low click-through rates, the title/meta isn't matching intent. If they have high clicks but poor time-on-page, the content isn't satisfying intent. Fix the underperformers.

**24. Is each article at least 1,500 words?**

Articles under 1,500 words rarely satisfy informational search intent for competitive keywords. Identify articles below this threshold and expand them or merge them with related thin articles.

**25. Do articles cover the expected entities for their topic area?**

Use the entity audit framework from [Semantic SEO and Entity-Based Optimization](/blog/semantic-seo-entity-optimization): identify the 15–20 core entities in your topic area and verify each article addresses the relevant ones.

**26. Are article headlines specific and keyword-aligned?**

Headlines that are vague or clever rather than specific and keyword-aligned reduce relevance signals. "5 Things to Know About Topical Authority" is weaker than "How Google Actually Evaluates Topical Expertise (And What Triggers It)."

**27. Do articles use evidence, data, and specific examples?**

Content that makes claims without supporting them with specific data, examples, or evidence is lower quality by Google's EEAT standards. Audit for the presence of concrete evidence in each article.

**28. Are articles updated when the subject matter changes?**

Outdated content is a negative EEAT signal. Review your cluster articles for outdated statistics, tool recommendations, or strategy advice that no longer reflects current best practices.

**29. Does the content reflect original expertise or is it generic?**

Topical authority strategy emphasizes demonstrating original expertise — using your own data, case studies, and specific operational experience. Generic "what the research says" content is weaker than "here's what we observed in our own cluster." Audit for authenticity.

**30. Is the reading level appropriate for your target audience?**

Content too technical for a beginner audience and content too basic for an expert audience both underperform on quality signals. Verify your articles match the expertise level of readers likely to search those queries.

## Section 5: Technical Health (Questions 31–35)

**31. Are all cluster articles indexed?**

Verify via Google Search Console URL Inspection or a `site:yourdomain.com` search for each article. Unindexed articles contribute nothing to the cluster signal.

**32. Do cluster pages load in under 3 seconds on mobile?**

Page speed is a ranking factor. Measure using Google PageSpeed Insights for each cluster article. Fix critical performance issues on high-impression pages first.

**33. Is there a clean canonical structure across the cluster?**

Duplicate content or incorrect canonical tags can split authority signals. Verify that each cluster article has a unique canonical URL and no competing duplicate pages.

**34. Is your sitemap updated with all cluster articles?**

An updated XML sitemap helps Google discover and crawl new cluster articles faster. Verify your sitemap includes all cluster article URLs.

**35. Does your robots.txt or noindex configuration correctly expose all cluster articles?**

A common technical mistake: accidentally noindexing category pages or tag archives that contain cluster content, which prevents crawlers from following internal links to cluster articles. Verify no cluster content is inadvertently blocked.

## Section 6: Performance Metrics (Questions 36–40)

**36. Are you tracking all 6 topical authority progress metrics?**

See [Measuring Topical Authority Progress: The 6 Metrics That Actually Matter](/blog/measuring-topical-authority-progress) for the complete tracking framework. Without systematic measurement, you can't diagnose or fix what's not working.

**37. Are low-KD foundational articles (KD 15–30) ranking on page 1 or 2 after 8 weeks?**

If your foundational low-KD articles haven't reached page-1 or page-2 after 8 weeks, the cluster has a systematic problem. Diagnose before publishing more content.

**38. Is average position improving week-over-week across the cluster?**

A cluster with growing topical authority should show consistent average position improvement, even if individual articles fluctuate. Flat or worsening average position after week 6 signals a coverage or quality issue.

**39. Is cluster impression volume growing 15%+ week-over-week during the sprint?**

Impression growth is the most reliable leading indicator of topical authority development. Below 15% week-over-week growth during active publication indicates a coverage gap or indexing problem.

**40. Have any competitive keywords (KD 35+) entered page-1 rankings after 10 weeks?**

If no mid-difficulty keywords are ranking on page 1 after 10 weeks of a complete cluster, audit content depth and competitive landscape. You may be in a category where the authority threshold is higher than expected.

## Audit Scoring and Priority Actions

**Count your "No" answers by section:**

| Section | "No" Count | Priority |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Architecture (1–8) | 3+ | Critical — fix before publishing more content |
| Keyword Strategy (9–16) | 2+ | High — affects entire cluster performance |
| Internal Linking (17–22) | 2+ | High — fix immediately |
| Content Quality (23–30) | 3+ | Medium — prioritize high-impression articles first |
| Technical Health (31–35) | 1+ | High — technical issues block all other signals |
| Performance Metrics (36–40) | 2+ | Medium — update measurement before next sprint |

**High-priority action items** (from Architecture and Technical Health sections) must be addressed before you publish more content. Publishing more articles on top of a broken cluster architecture doesn't fix the architecture — it deepens the problem.

## What to Do After This Audit

If this audit has revealed significant gaps, here's the prioritized action sequence:

1. **Fix technical issues first** (Section 5) — indexing problems block everything else
2. **Complete the cluster architecture** (Section 1) — add a pillar if missing, fill coverage gaps
3. **Repair internal links** (Section 3) — update pillar links, add links between supporting articles
4. **Expand low-KD coverage** (Section 2) — add foundational articles if keyword distribution is too high
5. **Improve thin content** (Section 4) — expand articles below 1,500 words before adding new ones
6. **Set up metrics tracking** (Section 6) — you can't improve what you're not measuring
